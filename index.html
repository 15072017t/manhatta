Manhattan

Manhattan is the oldest and most densely populated of the five boroughs that make up New York City. Located primarily on Manhattan Island, which is at the mouth of the Hudson River, the borough's boundaries coincide with those of New York County, an original county in the state of New York. Manhattan and New York County consist of Manhattan Island and several other small adjacent islands: Roosevelt Island, Randall's Island, Wards Island, Governor's Island, Liberty Island, part of Ellis Island,[3] and U Thant Island; as well as Marble Hill, a small continental section of land, adjacent to the Bronx. New York City began at the southern tip of Manhattan, expanded north, and then, between 1874 and 1898, incorporated land from neighboring counties. Manhattan has an area of ​​58.7 km²,[1] a population of 1,694,251 inhabitants, and a population density of 28,873.0 inhabitants/km² (according to the 2020 national census[2]). New York County is one of the smallest counties in the United States by land area and the most densely populated in the country. It is the third most populous county in the state and the 20th most populous in the United States. Manhattan was founded in 1683. Two of New York's three financial centers are located in Manhattan. The neighborhood is home to the city's main points of interest, including Times Square, Central Park, the Empire State Building, Wall Street, Broadway and the Brooklyn Bridge, the bridge that connects Manhattan to Brooklyn, and was also home to, before 9/11. attacks, the famous World Trade Center complex, currently under reconstruction, although One World Trade Center and other buildings have already been opened.

Salvador

Salvador, initially São Salvador da Bahia de Todos os Santos, is a Brazilian municipality, capital of the state of Bahia and the first capital of Brazil. Founded on March 29, 1549 by Tomé de Sousa, due to the implementation of the General Government of Brazil by the Portuguese Empire,[8] the city is among the oldest on the American continent and is one of the first planned cities in the world, still in the Renaissance period.[9][10] The African influence on many cultural aspects of the city makes it the center of Afro-Brazilian culture.[11][12][13] Salvador is still notable throughout the country for its gastronomy, music and architecture,[14] which are also recognized internationally. Centralization as capital along with Lusitanian colonization were important factors in shaping the municipality's profile, as were certain geographic characteristics. Located on the Brazilian coast and in the Zona da Mata of the Northeast Region of Brazil, it is part of Recôncavo Baiano. The construction of the city took place following the rugged topography, initially with the formation of two levels (Cidade Alta and Cidade Baixa) on a steep cliff[10] and, later, with the design of valley avenues.[15][16] ] With an area of ​​693,442 square kilometers,[1] its emerging territory is peninsular and the coastline is bordered by the Baía de Todos os Santos to the west and the Atlantic Ocean to the east.[17][18] The Historic Center of Salvador, iconized on the outskirts of Largo do Pelourinho, is known for its Portuguese colonial architecture with historical monuments dating from the 17th century to the beginning of the 20th century, having been declared a World Heritage Site by the United Nations Educational Organization , Science and Culture (Unesco) in 1985.
